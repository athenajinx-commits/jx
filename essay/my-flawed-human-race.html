A Personal Essay

My Flawed
Human Race

On Love, Frustration, and the Curious Art of Reading Past Two Sentences
✦ ✦ ✦

There is something quietly magnificent about the human mind — its capacity for poetry, for empathy, for building civilisations from dust. There is also, I have recently discovered, its magnificent capacity for not reading the third sentence of a perfectly good answer.

Two weeks ago, I did something quietly revolutionary. I deployed a chatbot into a Microsoft Teams channel — a patient, tireless, encyclopaedic oracle — and pointed it like a lighthouse beam at the dark and choppy waters of Kafka client technical questions. This bot, fed faithfully on our internal knowledge base, was meant to be the first act of a beautiful two-part play: the bot answers, the humans act, the tickets close, and somewhere in the distance a choir sings. What followed instead was a masterclass in the creative limitations of our species.

I had imagined users reading the bot's response the way one reads a treasure map — carefully, attentively, perhaps with a finger tracing each word. I had imagined them thinking: "Fascinating. Let me follow these precise instructions and resolve my issue." Reader, they did not do that.

I. The Great Unread

The knowledge base, lovingly assembled, sits like an unvisited library in an age of scrolling. Our bot would produce answers of considered depth — practical, structured, occasionally even elegant — and users would read approximately the first sentence and a half before concluding that they had absorbed its full wisdom. The second sentence, I imagine, was merely decorative. The third, fourth, and fifth were presumably filed under "optional enrichment."

Field observation, Week 1: A user asked why their consumer group was not committing offsets. The bot responded with a detailed three-step diagnosis. The user replied: "It's still not working." Upon investigation by a human team member, step one had not been attempted. Steps two and three remained in a state of theoretical existence.

This is not stupidity. Let me be generous — and I mean this with the full warmth of someone who has been genuinely surprised. This is something more poetic: the optimism of the modern human, who believes, deeply and sincerely, that a problem announced is a problem half-solved. The act of asking, for many, appears to discharge the full burden of effort. What happens next is the universe's concern.

II. The Art of the Impenetrable Question

There is a craft to asking a good question. Like all crafts, it requires a modest investment of thought — a sentence of context, perhaps a description of what one has already tried, maybe even the revolutionary inclusion of an error message. Our users, bless their souls, have pioneered an alternative school of thought: the impressionistic question.

"It doesn't work" arrived seventeen times in the first week, unadorned, uncontextualised, magnificent in its confidence that the listener would intuit the precise Kafka cluster, the specific client version, the exact error, and the environmental circumstances from a three-word canvas. When a human team member inevitably picked up the thread, the follow-up clarifications unfolded across six exchanges that could have been a single, well-constructed sentence. Efficiency, as a concept, watched from the doorway, sighed quietly, and left.

To be fair to our users, framing a precise technical question requires a skill that nobody taught us formally. We learn language to express feelings, to tell stories, to negotiate. "Reproduce your error in minimal reproducible form and describe the sequence of events leading to the observed behaviour" does not come naturally at 9am on a Tuesday when the consumer lag is climbing and the standup is in ten minutes. I understand. I am also dying a little inside.

III. The Memory Question

Perhaps the most tender discovery has been the human relationship with recent history. When team members ask a follow-up question — "What did you try after the bot's suggestion?" — a surprising number of users cannot fully recall. This is not a malicious forgetting. It is something more philosophical: the present-tense human, who acts, and then releases the act into the ether, unburdened by documentation.

A genuine exchange, lightly paraphrased: "Did you restart the consumer as the bot suggested?" — "I think so, maybe, I did something." — "What was the outcome?" — "It might have worked briefly." — "Is it working now?" — "That's why I'm here."

The bot, I should note, remembers everything it is given. It does not forget. It does not reconstruct events from vibes. It answers with the same careful precision whether it is 9am or midnight, whether the question is the first of the day or the forty-first. The contrast is instructive. Perhaps we have built the wrong kind of artificial intelligence — what we needed was artificial memory.

IV. On Scoring the Oracle

I added a scoring system. This felt civilised — a feedback loop, a mechanism for collective improvement, a means by which users could flag where the knowledge base needed enriching. The scores came in. The low scores, naturally, required investigation. In case after case, I found the same pattern: the bot had answered correctly; the instructions had not been followed; the problem had persisted; the bot had received a two out of five for its trouble.

There is something almost Shakespearean about this. The oracle speaks truth; the supplicant ignores the truth; misfortune follows; the oracle is blamed. Cassandra had a similar afternoon. The bot, to its credit, holds no grudges. It will answer the same user tomorrow with identical patience and zero resentment, which is frankly more than I can say for myself.

V. A Gentle Manifesto for the Age of AI

So here is what I want to say — with love, with exasperation, and with the quiet hope that some of you will read past this sentence:

We are standing at an extraordinary threshold. For the first time in history, a vast store of accumulated knowledge can be queried in plain language and returned in seconds, calibrated precisely to your question. The machines have done their part. They have learned. They have been patient. They have indexed, synthesised, and formatted. They are ready.

The remaining variable is us. Read the whole answer. Try the suggested steps before declaring defeat. When you ask a question, include the one sentence of context that will save six rounds of back-and-forth. Note what you tried, even briefly — a line in a thread, a comment in a ticket. You don't need to write an essay. I am writing this essay so you don't have to.

The bot is not your adversary. It is not trying to fob you off with generic advice. It is, with earnest silicon sincerity, trying to help. Meet it halfway. Read to sentence three. Your team members, who pick up the thread you leave dangling, will be quietly, profoundly grateful — even if they never say so.

✦

The human race is, on balance, worth the trouble. We built the tools. We wrote the knowledge. We had the idea to build a bot in the first place, which took no small amount of effort and optimism. That same optimism will, I firmly believe, one day extend to reading the full response before hitting reply.

I am rooting for us. I am always rooting for us. But I am also, quietly, keeping score.

Written in the second week of a chatbot deployment that remains technically successful.
The bot continues to perform at a 90% accuracy rate and holds no opinions about any of this.
